Question (b): Lowpass Filter Cutoff Frequency Effects
Three experiments show the effect of filter cutoff frequency:

1. Too Low (1 kHz cutoff):
   - The recovered signal is heavily smoothed and loses all high-frequency message content
   - cannot track fast changes in the message

2. Too High (50 kHz cutoff):
   - The carrier ripples and high-frequency noise pass through the filter
   - The output is very noisy with visible oscillations at carrier frequency (15 kHz)
   - The signal is recovered but with unacceptable noise 

3. Just Right (15 kHz cutoff):
   - The filter removes the carrier while preserving message content
   - The output is clean with minimal ripple and noise
   - This is the optimal tradeoff between noise reduction and signal fidelity

Principle: The cutoff should be slightly above the highest message frequency to prevent aliasing 
and loss of information, while being well below the carrier frequency to attenuate carrier ripples.

Question (c): Envelope Detector Applied to DSB-SC - Why It Fails
When the envelope detector is applied to DSB-SC (suppressed carrier), the output is incorrect.

Why it fails:
1. When m(t) is negative, the modulated signal just reverses polarity (phase modulation)
2. The envelope detector only tracks positive peaks (or absolute peaks in a rectifier)
3. Therefore, it recovers |m(t)| instead of m(t), losing negative values 

Question (d): Advantages and Limitations of Envelope Detection

Advantages:
1. Simplicity: Requires only (passive components) 
2. Low complexity: No need for coherent demodulation, synchronization, or local oscillator generation

Limitations:
1. Power Inefficiency: DSB-LC wastes a lot of power in the carrier component
2. Fails on overmodulation: Breaks down completely if μ > 1 (envelope goes negative)
3. Cannot demodulate general DSB-SC or SSB signals
